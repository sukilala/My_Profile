Project Idea - Carbon Dome a Martian Habitat


Overview


The following paper will attempt to describe the concept in everyday language, keeping numbers and jargon to a minimum. Inspired by the recent 3-D printed habitat challenge presented by NASA, Structural Member Competition (Phase 2). Even such submissions to the challenge are now closed. The concept described below will attempt to provide a technologically and economically feasible method to solve the problem in establishing a permanent Martian habitat.



Motivation


So What is Carbon Dome Anyway?


The only way to thrive on Mars and not just survive, short of terraforming is to build a self-sustaining habitat on the Martian surface, but transporting the necessary resources to build such a habit would be far too uneconomical amongst a host of other problems. So this is when things get tricky.


Description


One of the most innovative methods provided to solve this problem, is 3D printing. The idea goes as- by harvesting regolith or other forms of solid matter from the martian soil we could 3D print a Martian habitat layer by layer, but most methods are at best theoretical, as even to this day we still know very little about the composition of the martian soil.


But not to worry we have a proven natural resource on Mars we can use to build our habitat, carbon dioxide, yes, now I know what you may be thinking- carbon dioxide is a gas, how can we use it to build a solid structure? Not to worry I have got the answer, by using a system that uses electrolytic syntheses, the process provided below can make carbon nanofibers from atmospheric air, in this case, Mars. Here's how it will work:


  1. To power, the synthesis, heat, and electricity are produced through a hybrid and extremely efficient concentrating solar-energy system. The system focuses the sun's rays on a photovoltaic solar cell to generate electricity and on a second system to generate heat and thermal energy, which raises the temperature of an electrolytic cell.

  2. CO2 is broken down in a high-temperature electrolytic bath of molten carbonates at 1,380 degrees F (750 degrees C).

  3. Atmospheric air is added to an electrolytic cell.

  4. The CO2 dissolves when subjected to the heat and direct current through electrodes of nickel and steel. The carbon nanofibers build up on the steel electrode, where they can be removed.

The following method was developed by a team of researchers at the George Washington University, presumably as a way to lower the potent greenhouse gas from our own atmosphere at the same time making it economical, but I realised a different yet not entirely different use for this technology which I will discuss in more detail further down the paper.

Tools and Technologies, Skills Required & Outcome

Feasibility

  • Where would the power come from? Granted that the research paper does not explicitly say how energy intensive the process would be, but one of the lead researchers, Lindt estimates it would cost 1000 dollars worth of electricity per ton of nanofibers, considering the average cost per kilowatt of electricity is 10 cents, it would take 10000 kilowatt-hours to produce one ton worth of carbon nanofibres. The maximum solar irradiance on Mars is about 590 W/m2 compared to about 1000 W/m2 at the Earth's surface. Also, year-round dust storms on Mars may block sunlight for weeks at a time. In Equatorial regions such as the Martian Pavonis Mons, which is a great spot for this dome by the way but more on that later, receives about 12 hours worth of daylight hours per day, that means a solar array with an area of 100 square metres and an efficiency of 30 percent, would produce 212 kilowatt hours per day, which translates to 21 kilograms worth of carbon nanofibres per day. Now let's go back to the dust storm problem let's imagine every second day of the week blocks the sun entirely making the system inert, unlikely but not impossible, at that rate, we would produce 38 tons worth of carbon nanofibres within 10 years.


  • Where would we build it? On top of a lava tube entrance of course, where else? This image below of a lava tube skylight entrance on the Martian volcano Pavonis Mons is only about 35 meters in diameter and the perfect candidate for our 3D printed dome. Lava tubes solve a host of problems that come with colonization of Mars such as dangerous levels of cosmic radiation that may be detrimental to the colonists long term health, gives a constant temperature forum in otherwise chaotic temperature fluctuations on top of the surface but they still lack one thing that is most dire, safe atmospheric pressure - that's where our dome comes into play, our dome would be 35 metres in diameter, 11.6 metres in height, has a thickness of 1 metre, and will weigh 35 tons.

Possible lava tube entrance observed by HiRISE Diameter: 35m - Credits: NASA


  • How would we build it? The exact size of the system used to build the dome is not available but I presume it would not be much bigger than the mars rover and can come folded. Before we start printing the dome, screw pile steel foundation will need to be installed, this can be done using an autonomous rover, the steps as follows;

  1. A small rover will clear the dust till, solid ground is visible, around the lava tube.

  2. Another rover will then place a steel plate and then drive steel tubular piles into the ground by a simple jackhammer and attachment tool, then a specially designed post and cap plate for easy fixing to bearer will be attached, 4 of these would be more than enough to handle the load of the dome.

  3. Then the carbon dioxide to carbon nanofibres system and the carbon nanofiber 3d printer, will start the printing process- first it will print around the edge of the lava tube using a rover, then slowly keep printing all the way to the top using a flywheel like system for about 10 years until the dome is complete.
